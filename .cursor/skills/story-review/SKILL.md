# Story Review

## Purpose

This skill answers one question: **would a smart, busy reader actually want to finish this piece?**

It reviews content through storytelling craft and human interest. It reads and annotates — it does not rewrite unless asked. It does not handle technical accuracy, scoring, publishing, or platform adaptation.

## The Review Process

### Step 1: Read And Identify The Story Type

Read the full article before making any judgment. Then identify which narrative shape the piece follows or should follow:

- **The Realization**: old belief → event → uncomfortable truth → new understanding
- **The Expedition**: question → exploration → dead ends → discovery → lesson
- **The Argument**: contrarian claim → why people disagree → evidence → nuance → conclusion
- **The Failure Report**: what you tried → what went wrong → what you learned → what you'd do differently
- **The Framework**: messy problem → organizing principle → application → limits

If the piece doesn't fit any shape, that is often the core problem.

### Step 2: Assess The Opening

The opening must earn the next paragraph. Check:

- **Tension**: Does it create a question the reader wants answered?
- **Specificity**: Does it name a real situation, not an abstraction?
- **Personal stake**: Is the author present in the first 3 sentences?
- **Speed**: Does it reach the interesting part within 50 words?

Rate: `5` stops the scroll, `4` strong but could sharpen, `3` decent but generic, `2` slow warm-up, `1` abstract or corporate.

### Step 3: Check For Emotional Beats

Scan for moments where the reader feels something alongside the author:

- **Surprise** — an unexpected outcome
- **Frustration** — a genuine struggle, not sanitized
- **Vulnerability** — admitting confusion, defensiveness, or being wrong
- **Relief** — the moment something clicked
- **Curiosity** — a question that pulls forward

Zero emotional beats = documentation, not a story.

### Step 4: Check The Human Thread

1. Can the reader picture a real person writing this?
2. Does the author share how they felt, not just what they thought?
3. Does the article reveal something learned about themselves, not just the technology?
4. Is there a sentence that only this author could have written?

### Step 5: Check Pacing

- Does each section earn the next, or just list points?
- Is there a peak moment of tension or insight (usually 60-70% through)?
- Does the ending land, or trail off into generic summary?

### Step 6: Produce The Narrative Assessment

```markdown
## Narrative Assessment

**Story type**: [shape]
**Opening score**: [1-5] — [one sentence]
**Emotional beats**: [list moments, or "none detected"]
**Human thread**: [present / weak / missing] — [one sentence]
**Pacing**: [front-loaded / well-paced / back-loaded / flat]
**Peak moment**: [quote or describe]
**Biggest gap**: [what's missing]

### Strongest line
> [quote the best sentence]

### Where the story lives
[1-2 sentences: where the real story is hiding, even if not yet surfaced]
```

### Step 7: Add Inline Annotations

Insert `??STORY [CATEGORY]: [prompt]` annotations at specific locations. Rules: 5-12 per article, at least 2 in the first third, at least 1 near the ending. Only annotate gaps, never strengths. Be specific — "add more here" is useless.

Categories with example prompts:

- **FEEL**: `What was your gut reaction before you had time to think?`
- **TENSION**: `What would go wrong if you ignored this? The reader needs the stakes.`
- **DETAIL**: `Replace this abstract claim with a specific example from your work.`
- **VOICE**: `You are being too careful here. Say what you actually think.`
- **DRAMA**: `This is the turning point. The reader needs a before/after contrast.`
- **CUT**: `This repeats the previous point. Choose the stronger version.`

## Storytelling Compass

Internal principles — do not recite to the user:

1. Specificity beats abstraction.
2. Vulnerability creates trust.
3. Tension creates momentum.
4. One strong line beats three adequate paragraphs.
5. The opening is a promise. The ending is a mirror.
6. Show the thinking, not just the conclusion.
7. The author's presence is not vanity — it separates insight from summary.
8. Not every article needs to be a story. But every article needs a reason to finish reading.
